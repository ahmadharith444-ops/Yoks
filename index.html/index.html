Owl Species in Malaysia

Listen • Learn • Recognise

Sunda Scops-Owl (Otus lempiji)

This is one of the most commonly heard owls in Malaysia, especially around villages, orchards, and forest edges. Despite its small size, the Sunda Scops-Owl is extremely adaptable and comfortable living close to human-modified landscapes. Most people recognise it by its repetitive, high-pitched call that echoes through the night.

It feeds mainly on insects and small invertebrates, making it an important natural pest controller. If you hear a short, steady call after sunset, chances are this owl is nearby.

Buffy Fish Owl (Ketupa ketupu)

The Buffy Fish Owl is a large and powerful owl often found near rivers, wetlands, and forested waterways. Unlike many owls, this species feeds heavily on fish, frogs, and other aquatic animals. Its presence usually indicates a healthy water ecosystem.

This owl has a deep, booming call that sounds almost haunting in quiet riverine forests. Because it depends on clean water and natural riverbanks, habitat destruction poses a serious threat to this species.

Oriental Bay Owl (Phodilus badius)

This owl is rarely seen but often heard in dense forests and shaded plantations. The Oriental Bay Owl has a distinctive heart-shaped face, similar to the Barn Owl, but prefers more forested environments.

Its call is soft, eerie, and ghost-like, making it one of the most mysterious owls in Malaysia. It feeds on small mammals and insects and plays a quiet but important role in forest ecosystems.

Brown Boobook (Ninox scutulata)

The Brown Boobook is a small, agile owl commonly found in lowland forests, gardens, and agricultural areas. It is highly vocal and often calls repeatedly from the same perch.

This species feeds on insects, small birds, and rodents. Its sharp, rhythmic call makes it relatively easy to identify once you are familiar with the sound.

Owl Call:

Brown Wood Owl (Strix leptogrammica)

This large forest owl prefers mature forests with tall trees. The Brown Wood Owl is rarely seen during the day and usually roosts high in dense canopy cover.

Its deep, resonant hoots can travel long distances at night. The species depends heavily on intact forest habitats, making deforestation a major concern for its survival.

Owl Call:

Collared Scops Owl (Otus lettia)

This species is rarely detected in Malaysia and is known for its sporadic appearances. The Collared Scops Owl prefers forested habitats and is extremely quiet outside its short calling periods.

Due to its low detection rate, very little is known by the public about this owl. Hearing its call is often considered a rare and lucky experience.

Owl Call:

Barn Owl (Tyto alba)

The Barn Owl is one of the most recognisable owls in Malaysia, especially in oil palm plantations and agricultural landscapes. It is widely used as a natural rodent control agent.

Unlike typical hooting owls, the Barn Owl produces a harsh, screeching sound. Its silent flight and efficient hunting skills make it a key ally for farmers.

Owl Call:

Barred Eagle-Owl (Bubo sumatranus)

This is one of the largest owl species in Malaysia and a true apex nocturnal predator. It inhabits forest edges and mature woodlands.

The Barred Eagle-Owl has a powerful, booming call and feeds on a wide range of prey, including mammals and other birds. Due to its size, it requires large territories to survive.

Owl Call:

Spotted Wood Owl (Strix seloputo)

The Spotted Wood Owl is a forest-associated species often found near forest margins and mixed landscapes. It is less common and generally avoids highly disturbed areas.

Its call is deep and rhythmic, usually heard late at night. The species depends on habitat connectivity and forest complexity to thrive.
